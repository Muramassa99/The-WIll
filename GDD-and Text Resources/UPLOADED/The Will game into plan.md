# 1) CUTSCENE STORYBOARD VERSION

## THE WILL — Opening Sequence / First Death / Forge Introduction

---

## Scene 0 — Black before the world

**Purpose:** establish tone before clarity

**Visual**

* Black screen
* faint wind
* distant stone resonance, almost like something old humming in its sleep
* no music yet, only low atmosphere
* a few slow, heavy sounds, like something vast settling under pressure

**Text option, if any is used at all**
Very minimal. No lore paragraph. No exposition wall.

If any opening text exists, it should be something short and suggestive, not explanatory, for example:

* *Some places are built.*
* *Some places survive.*
* *And some places should never have existed at all.*

Then fade in.

---

## Scene 1 — The Town, alive before the player understands it

**Purpose:** make Town feel lived-in, real, and slightly strange

**Visual**

* the player’s first glimpse of Town
* people moving through it with purpose
* stonework that feels old but maintained
* distant gates visible in the environment, never fully centered at first, but present enough to matter
* market-like motion, guild traffic, routine life
* no one acting like the place is mysterious, because for them this is daily life

**Tone**
The Town should feel:

* inhabited
* resilient
* familiar to its residents
* slightly burdened
* built around things older than the people using them

**Important**
Nothing should scream:

> “this is the anomaly hub of overlapping realities”

Not yet.

It should first feel normal enough to be trusted.

---

## Scene 2 — Player receives first direction

**Purpose:** ease the player into the lie

The player is guided to the Guild Hall or equivalent front-facing quest hub.

At the counter is **Gerald Ston**, known by the locals as **Mister Ston**.

### Gerald Ston visual/personality direction

* male
* around his 40s
* former adventurer
* now desk-bound
* practical
* worn down, but not broken
* the sort of man who speaks like someone who has seen enough to stop wasting words
* does not feel evil
* does not feel cartoonishly deceptive
* feels like a man who does what needs doing and does not always say everything

He looks the player over and notices the lack of real gear.

He sends them to take basic equipment from a storage chest.

He gives:

* a map
* a journal
* a simple-sounding task

This first task should read like:

* a proving errand
* a starter outing
* something beneath suspicion

It is the opening lie.

---

## Scene 3 — Gear, map, and false simplicity

**Purpose:** set the player up to believe the world is just beginning

The player accesses the starter chest.

Possible environmental cues:

* gear that looks practical, not heroic
* signs that this is communal issue equipment
* not enough to inspire confidence, but enough that a newcomer might think:
  *this should do*

The player receives:

* a map
* a journal

The map/journal framing should imply:

* not everything is fully charted
* people still rely on what is brought back
* this is a place where knowledge is accumulated by those willing to go out and return

This is important because it quietly seeds one of the core laws of the game:
**knowledge matters**

---

## Scene 4 — The gate and the first step out

**Purpose:** begin the movement from comfort toward scale

The player approaches the gate.

This is the first time the gate should be given real visual weight.

**Visual notes**

* ancient stone arch
* heavier than the buildings around it
* older than the people
* not decorative
* physically solid enough to feel like it is holding something in place, not just opening a door

The player passes through.

This is not yet the moment for deep explanation.
The world beyond should do the explaining through contrast.

---

## Scene 5 — The Floor as a place, not a room

**Purpose:** make the player understand this is not a starter dungeon

The first Floor should feel bigger than the task that sent the player there.

The player is meant to feel:

* uncertainty
* under-prepared curiosity
* that the place does not care why they came

The world should feel like:

* it already existed before the player entered it
* it is not waiting for the player specifically
* the quest objective is small compared to the scale of the place

Exploration should naturally teach:

* movement
* space-reading
* caution
* the sense that things live here

No giant tutorial walls.
No overexplaining.
The place teaches by being itself.

---

## Scene 6 — The overheard truth (optional / missable / timed world event)

**Purpose:** seed the fuse through world rumor, not tutorial delivery

This is not a fixed cutscene beat for every player.
This is a **possible missable overheard event**.

At the right place, right hour, right day, the player may overhear two middle-to-elderly women talking nearby.

They are not talking *to* the player.
They are talking *about* what people are doing, what Mister Ston keeps sending young ones toward, and how much he does not say.

This is the **boycotting dialogue layer**.

Their lines should suggest:

* that Ston is sending players into something larger than he admits
* that the task is not what it sounds like
* that the deeper thing on that Floor does not stir for nothing
* that “those three great beasts” or “those three behemoths” or equivalent must be brought low before the deeper imbalance appears
* that this is known in fragments by older Town residents
* that the young do not always listen long enough to understand what they are walking into

Important:

* do **not** mention a final boss directly
* do **not** explain mechanics like a wiki
* do **not** sound like an MMO strategy guide
* the dialogue should feel like old frustration, old gossip, and practical warning

The point is not tutorialization.
The point is infection.

One player hearing it is enough.

---

## Scene 7 — Collapse

**Purpose:** first death becomes the real threshold

Eventually the player dies.

This death must feel:

* inevitable
* instructive
* bigger than personal failure
* like the world simply proved something to them

This is not a “you lose” sting.
This is initiation.

---

## Scene 8 — Blackout and return glimpse

**Purpose:** first contact with the deeper rules

**Visual sequence**

* black
* breath
* heavy ringing / muffled distortion
* eyes open briefly
* the player sees part of a return circle or extraction effect
* eyes close
* eyes open again

Now there is a face too close, filling vision.

An old man.

Not Gerald.

Someone older.

Someone who feels like he belongs more deeply to Town than to the counter.

He is the one who begins the real explanation.

---

## Scene 9 — The old man’s walk-and-talk

**Purpose:** reframe the world without fully explaining it

This conversation should not be delivered in one static lore block.

Better:

* the old man helps the player up
* he begins speaking while moving
* he leads the player toward the central forge gate
* the player hears the explanation in fragments while walking

This is where the player learns:

* lasting that long was unusual with that gear
* someone probably got them out
* the fact they still have what they gathered matters
* normally, if you go down beyond the gate, you lose your hold on that place
* and what belongs there tends to stay there
* if someone got you back in time, you owe them more than thanks
* the gates are older than the town
* no one truly knows who made them
* some of the younger minds from earlier generations had theories — parallel worlds, other timelines, broken realities — but he never cared much for those words
* what matters is this: people go out, people come back, and those that do not come back properly lose more than blood

The old man then leads the player to the Forge.

---

## Scene 10 — Forge reveal

**Purpose:** reveal the real answer to the first failure

The Forge should not be introduced as:

* a feature
* a crafting bench
* a system unlock

It should feel like:

* a hidden necessity
* a place the older world depends on
* something stable where unstable matter can be made useful
* a door into the next layer of understanding

The old man does not explain it perfectly.

He sends the player in.

His body language should say:

* he knows enough to send people there
* not enough to explain everything
* or not enough patience to do so

That makes the Forge more powerful, not less.

---

## Scene 11 — End of intro / beginning of real game

**Purpose:** hand off from narrative to agency

The player is now allowed to enter the Forge.

This marks the true beginning of The Will.

Not because the game started now,
but because the player now understands:

* the world is bigger than it looked
* they are weaker than they assumed
* survival and success are not the same thing
* the Gates matter
* the Forge matters
* what they build will matter

Fade into Forge gameplay onboarding.

# 2) QUEST-SYSTEM IMPLEMENTATION SHEET

## THE WILL — Opening Quest / World Ignition / Forge Unlock

---

## 2.1 System intent

The first quest is not a standard starter quest.

It is a **two-layer system**:

### Layer A — player-facing surface

A simple first outing:

* talk to NPC
* get gear
* get map/journal
* go retrieve something

### Layer B — world-facing truth

A disguised world-scale progression trigger:

* hidden raid-equivalent objective
* server-wide opening condition
* social ignition mechanism
* first world-historical pressure point

This duality must be preserved.

---

## 2.2 Quest giver

**Name:** Gerald Ston
**Common name used by others:** Mister Ston

### Role

* former adventurer
* now guild hall counter / request issuer
* practical administrator
* later supports player-driven quest/request structure
* in opening phase acts as the one who sends the player into the disguised ignition condition

### Narrative behavior

* not openly villainous
* not overtly lying in a theatrical sense
* withholds scope
* frames the task as manageable
* may believe it needs doing, regardless of whether the newcomer fully understands it

---

## 2.3 Starter flow

### Step 1

Player talks to Gerald Ston.

### Step 2

Gerald notices the player’s lack of gear.

### Step 3

Player is directed to a storage chest.

### Step 4

Player takes basic starting gear.

### Step 5

Player receives:

* map
* journal

### Step 6

Player is told to go retrieve an item from beyond the gate.

### Step 7

Minimal UI guidance helps the player:

* navigate Town
* locate gate
* understand map/journal basics

Important:
UI guidance should support movement and comprehension,
not explain the hidden structure of the quest.

---

## 2.4 Hidden world condition behind the first quest

The actual progression structure on the first Floor is:

* a sought item or material is tied to a deeper event chain
* that deeper event requires the destabilization of the Floor
* the destabilization occurs when **three major local threats** are all brought down in overlapping time
* only then does the deeper consequence emerge
* the game should not speak of this in clean systems language early on

Internal implementation truth may be:

* 3 minibosses
* synchronized defeat window
* respawn timer
* spawn notification
* downstream unlock

But presentation truth should stay indirect.

---

## 2.5 Global unlock condition

### Floor progression law

* Floor 2 unlocks only after the first named Floor’s deeper target is beaten at least once
* this is a world/server condition
* not a private personal one
* later floors follow the same larger progression philosophy

This should be implemented as:

* world-state gate condition
* server-side progression flag
* update to gate access table after first successful clear

---

## 2.6 World-first achievement/title structure

The system must support first global clear identity.

### Required data structure support

For each named Floor or named deeper target:

* first global kill flag
* record of participants
* timestamp
* server/region context if applicable
* linked achievement/title unlock

### Long-term social title support

System should support:

* single first
* repeated firsts across different named targets
* stacking or escalating prestige titles later

The exact title wording can be refined later.

---

## 2.7 Missable information leak system

The game should support **rare overheard dialogue events**.

### Properties

* time-based
* day-based
* place-based
* proximity-based
* one-time or very low-frequency
* not formally announced
* not guaranteed for every player

### Use case

In the opening world push, one such event may involve:

* two older women
* gossiping / complaining / boycotting Mister Ston’s deception
* hinting that the “simple errand” is much larger than it sounds
* implying that three great presences must fall before something worse, deeper, or older stirs

### System purpose

This is not a tutorial trigger.
This is a **social contagion trigger**.

One player hearing it is enough.

---

## 2.8 First death trigger

The first death should trigger a special narrative sequence the first time only.

### Conditions

* first true death on first Floor
* or first qualifying death state beyond gate before Forge unlock

### Outcome

* interrupts ordinary death flow
* plays blackout/blink return scene
* hands off into old-man explanation
* unlocks Forge entry afterwards

---

## 2.9 Loot retention / extraction implication

The old man’s explanation implies a world rule:

* what is gathered beyond the gate belongs to that place until properly brought back
* losing consciousness usually breaks the player’s hold on that world
* failure to return properly can result in losing gathered materials
* return/extraction matters
* being helped by others matters

This should become a real long-term system principle, even if early implementation is staged gradually.

---

## 2.10 Forge unlock

Forge access should become available only after:

* first qualifying death sequence
* old-man walk-and-talk
* approach to the Forge gate

This makes Forge entry feel like:

* a consequence
* a necessity
* a next step in world understanding

not just a feature menu opening.

---

## 2.11 What the player should understand by the end of startup

By the end of this opening chain, the player should understand:

* the Town is home, but not simple
* the gates are important and old
* the world beyond them is larger than the player
* starter gear is not enough for everything
* failure can still teach and still matter
* what you bring back matters
* the Forge is the answer to not being enough yet
* other people know more than they openly say

That is the minimum successful outcome.

# 3.2 Overheard NPC proximity dialogue draft

## Two older women, not directly addressing the player

This should play like:

* natural conversation
* annoyed familiarity
* local bitterness
* information leaking through complaint

### Version A — soft hint

**Woman 2:**
Mister Ston’s always had a way of cutting the edges off the truth. Says less, gets more done.

**Woman 2:**

Three times over, if memory still means anything.

# 3.5 Old man walk-and-talk draft

The old man helps the player up and begins walking slowly.

**Old Man:**
I’ll give you this much, young one…
I did not expect you to last that long out there wearing that.

**Old Man:**
And you still have what you gathered.
That’s the part I’m interested in.

**Old Man:**
Either you remembered to use a return scroll while your head was still yours…
or someone passing through saw fit to pull you back before the place swallowed the rest of you.

**Old Man:**
Be grateful for that, whichever it was.

**Old Man:**
Most who go down beyond the gate don’t just lose their footing. They lose their hold. Their sense. Their place in it. And when that happens, what they picked clean from there tends to stay there.

**Old Man:**
That world is not ours, young one.
Not truly.
What you carry out of it only stays with you if you come back properly.

**Old Man:**
So if someone sent you home with your bones and your haul both intact, remember it.
And do the same for another when your turn comes.

They keep walking.

**Old Man:**
The gates…
Mm.
Mysterious things.

**Old Man:**
These powders, the scrolls, the dust we scrape and save when we can… all of it comes from them in one way or another. Rare enough now. Can’t keep shaving old stone forever.

**Old Man:**
They were here before us.
Before the houses. Before the square. Before I was born and before my father’s father learned to walk bent.

**Old Man:**
Some of the younger minds who came before you had big words for it all. Parallel worlds. Other times. Fractured places leaning into one another.

**Old Man:**
Could be.
Could be all of it.
Could be worse.

**Old Man:**
I’ve lived long enough to know the gates lead somewhere that does not belong fully to us… and long enough to know they still keep something from becoming worse than it already is.

They arrive near the central forge gate.

**Old Man:**
Here.
This one.

**Old Man:**
The townsfolk call it the Forge.

**Old Man:**
Go inside.

**Old Man:**
You’ve learned the first lesson already, whether you meant to or not: wanting something from beyond the gate and being ready to take it are not the same thing.

**Old Man:**
If you’ve questions after that, ask someone younger. My bones are done with walking and my patience with explaining went years ago.

**Old Man:**
Take care of your back, too.
Carry too much too early and the world will bend you before it kills you.

**Old Man:**
Go on.
