My solution is to use negative space as a threshold-based balance system.

I do not want the game to smooth everything out evenly. I do not want all builds to slowly drift toward the same result through a constant scaling formula. That would flatten decision-making and make the system feel soft and artificial.

Instead, I want the balance to happen at clear usage categories.

The idea is that lower material usage keeps more bonuses, while higher material usage loses them in large, deliberate steps. This creates real decision points for the player. A player can choose to stay under a threshold and keep an important benefit, or go past it and accept losing that benefit in exchange for a different shape or design.

This means the system is not balanced around every single possible percentage value. It is balanced around specific anchor points. Those points become the places where real design choices happen.

Because of this, players will naturally try to stay just under important thresholds instead of going slightly over them. That is not a problem. That is the intended behavior. It adds one more layer of decision-making and optimization.

In other words, I am not trying to prevent players from squeezing value out of the system. I am designing the system so that squeezing value out of it is part of the game.

The goal is not to make every build equal. The goal is to make different build sizes able to reach similar endgame output through different paths.

A minimalist build keeps more bonuses because it uses less space. A fuller build gives up those bonuses because it uses more space. This allows both to remain competitive without making them feel the same.

The important part is that the changes should happen in noticeable steps, not in smooth linear scaling. If the system changes too smoothly, then players will not feel that they are making meaningful choices. If the system changes in clear stages, then every threshold becomes a real point of tension.

For example, if a player is close to the next category, they have to decide whether to redesign the weapon to stay under the current limit or push forward and accept the loss of a bonus. That is the kind of decision I want the system to create.

So the balance is not based on making all values equal across the whole range. The balance is based on making certain usage bands the real efficiency points of the system.

That is the core idea:
less used space keeps more bonuses,
more used space loses them,
and the meaningful decisions happen at the thresholds between those bands.

Examples:

A player makes a small, elegant blade and keeps several negative-space bonuses because the design stays under an important threshold. The weapon uses less material, but the bonuses help it stay competitive.

Another player makes a bigger, heavier weapon that crosses into the next usage band. The shape allows more material and more raw structure, but some bonuses are lost. The player accepted that trade on purpose.

A third player is very close to the next threshold and has to decide whether a few extra cells are worth losing a whole bonus. That moment is where the system becomes interesting.

Reminder:
I am not balancing for smooth fairness.
I am balancing for meaningful thresholds, intentional inefficiency between thresholds, and strong design decisions around those breakpoints.
